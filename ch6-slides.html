Chapter 6 · Hardware
Network Hardware
The physical devices that connect, direct, and amplify data across a network. Click each device to learn its job.
🔀
Router
Directs data packets between different networks — like a traffic cop deciding which road each packet takes. Your home router connects your LAN to the internet (WAN). Uses IP addresses for routing.
Click to reveal
🔌
Switch
Connects devices within the same network (LAN). Smarter than an old hub — it sends data only to the specific device it's meant for, not to everyone. Used in offices to connect computers.
Click to reveal
📡
Modem
Translates digital signals from your devices into signals that travel over cable/phone/fiber lines — and back. MOdulator-DEModulator. Connects your network to your ISP (internet service provider).
Click to reveal
📶
Access Point
Creates a wireless LAN by broadcasting a Wi-Fi signal. In a large building, multiple APs are placed in different areas so every room gets coverage. Different from a router — an AP extends an existing wired network wirelessly.
Click to reveal
Exam Trap Router ≠ Switch. A router connects different networks. A switch connects devices within the same network. Students confuse these every exam.

A law firm wants to connect 30 computers within their single office building so employees can share files. What type of network is this, and what hardware connects the computers?

Your Bluetooth headphones are paired to your phone. Your phone is on FIU's Wi-Fi. The Wi-Fi connects to the internet. Name the network type for each: headphone-to-phone, phone-to-FIU, FIU-to-internet.

A Comcast technician installs a box in your apartment that translates the cable signal into a signal your router can use. What device is this? What does its name stand for?
Modem — MOdulator/DEModulator. It converts (modulates) your router's digital signals into analog signals that travel over the cable line, and converts incoming signals back (demodulates). It bridges your home network to Comcast's network.

WAN (Wide Area Network) — spans multiple cities. The individual offices each have LANs, and a WAN connects them. Many organizations use a VPN (Virtual Private Network) over the internet to build a secure WAN between locations.

Chapter 6 · Protocols
TCP/IP — The Internet's Common Language
Without agreed-upon rules, devices can't communicate. Protocols are those rules. Click each to learn its role.
📦
IP Address
A unique numerical address assigned to every device on a network — like a street address for data. IPv4: 192.168.1.1 · IPv6: expanded to handle billions more devices as internet grew.
Click to reveal
✉️
TCP
Transmission Control Protocol — breaks data into packets, sends them, and ensures all packets arrive and are reassembled in order. Guarantees delivery. Used for email, web pages, file transfers where accuracy matters.
Click to reveal
🌐
HTTP/HTTPS
HyperText Transfer Protocol — governs how web browsers and servers exchange web pages. HTTPS adds encryption (SSL/TLS). The "S" is why your browser shows a padlock — data is encrypted in transit.
Click to reveal
📁
Other Protocols
FTP — File Transfer Protocol (moving files). SMTP/POP3/IMAP — email sending/receiving. DNS — converts "google.com" into an IP address. Each protocol = a specific set of rules for a specific job.
Click to reveal
Key Concept

TCP/IP is not one protocol — it's a suite of protocols. Think of it as a rule book for the internet. Every device from phones to servers follows the same rules, allowing them to communicate regardless of manufacturer.

Chapter 6 · Network Access
Internet · Intranet · Extranet
Same underlying technology — completely different access rules. This distinction is a guaranteed exam topic.
Feature 🌍 Internet 🏢 Intranet 🤝 Extranet
Who can access Anyone, worldwide Only internal employees Internal + selected partners
Purpose Global communication and commerce Internal collaboration and resources Collaboration with trusted outsiders
Security Open — public access Firewalled — private to org Controlled — selected access
Example FIU's public website (fiu.edu) FIU's internal staff portal (HR, payroll, internal docs) FIU sharing grade data with a government scholarship database
Exam Trap An intranet uses the same internet technology (TCP/IP, web browsers) — but it's private. It's NOT a separate physical network. The difference is access control, not technology.

Chapter 6 · Transmission
Bandwidth & Transmission Media
Bandwidth = how much data travels per second. Transmission media = what carries it. Click each to compare speeds and trade-offs.
🌊
Bandwidth
The capacity of a network connection — measured in bits per second (bps). Think of it as a pipe: wider pipe = more data flows. Mbps = megabits/sec. Gbps = gigabits/sec. More bandwidth = faster network.
Click to reveal
🔵
Fiber Optic
Fastest & most expensive. Glass fibers carry pulses of light. Speeds up to 400 Gbps. Immune to electromagnetic interference. Used for undersea cables, ISP backbone, and enterprise networks.
Click to reveal
🟡
Coaxial / Ethernet
Common wired options. Ethernet (twisted-pair copper) is in most offices — up to 10 Gbps. Coaxial cable is used for cable TV and internet (your Xfinity/Spectrum connection). Reliable, affordable, widely deployed.
Click to reveal
📡
Wireless
Wi-Fi, cellular (4G/5G), satellite. No cables — uses radio waves. Wi-Fi: up to ~9.6 Gbps (Wi-Fi 6). 5G: up to 10 Gbps. Satellite (Starlink): useful for remote areas. Trade-off: wireless can be slower and less secure than wired.
Click to reveal
Real World When Netflix buffers, it's usually a bandwidth problem — not enough capacity for your connection at that moment (too many users, too much data).

Chapter 6 · Wireless
Wireless Networking Standards
No wires — but very specific rules govern how devices communicate wirelessly. Click each standard to see what it does.
📶
Wi-Fi (802.11)
The standard for wireless LAN. 802.11 is the technical name; Wi-Fi is the brand. Wi-Fi 6 (802.11ax) reaches ~9.6 Gbps. Uses radio waves in 2.4 GHz (longer range, slower) and 5 GHz (shorter range, faster) bands. Range: ~100–300 feet indoors.
Click to reveal
🦷
Bluetooth
Short-range wireless PAN technology. Range: ~30 feet. Low power. Used for: earbuds, keyboards, speakers, medical devices, car audio. Not for internet — for direct device-to-device pairing. Named after a Viking king who united tribes.
Click to reveal
📲
5G Cellular
5th generation cellular network. Speeds up to 10 Gbps — 100x faster than 4G. Ultra-low latency (1ms). Enables: autonomous vehicles, remote surgery, smart cities, IoT at massive scale. Coverage still expanding (2024–2030 buildout).
Click to reveal
💳
NFC
Near Field Communication — range: ~4 cm. Used for: contactless payments (Apple Pay, Google Pay, tap credit cards), door access cards, transit passes. Very short range is a security feature — you must physically tap to transmit.
Click to reveal
Today in Class How many of these wireless standards are active on your phone right now? Wi-Fi + Bluetooth + 5G + NFC = 4 different radios operating simultaneously.

Chapter 6 · Security
VPN, Firewall & Network Security
Networks create convenience — and create attack surfaces. Click each security concept to see how it protects the network.
🔒
VPN
Virtual Private Network — creates an encrypted "tunnel" through the public internet. Your data is encrypted before leaving your device and decrypted at the destination. Used by: remote workers, businesses linking offices, users protecting privacy on public Wi-Fi.
Click to reveal
🧱
Firewall
Hardware or software that monitors and filters incoming/outgoing network traffic based on security rules. Acts as a gatekeeper — allows trusted traffic, blocks suspicious traffic. Every corporate network (and your home router) has a firewall.
Click to reveal
🎣
Network Threats
Packet sniffing: intercepting data on a network. Man-in-the-middle: attacker inserts themselves between sender and receiver. DoS/DDoS: flooding a network to crash it. Rogue Wi-Fi: fake hotspots that steal credentials. Why HTTPS matters everywhere.
Click to reveal
🌐
ISP & DNS
ISP (Internet Service Provider) — Comcast, AT&T — provides your internet connection. DNS (Domain Name System) — translates "fiu.edu" into an IP address. DNS is the internet's phone book. Without DNS, you'd need to memorize IP addresses for every website.

Chapter 6 · Takeaways
What You Must Know
  • Network scope: PAN (personal, ~10m) → LAN (building) → MAN (city) → WAN (global). The internet is the largest WAN. Know the scale and an example for each.
  • Hardware roles: Router = connects different networks using IP addresses. Switch = connects devices within the same LAN. Modem = translates signals between your network and ISP. These are three different devices with three different jobs.
  • TCP/IP is a suite of protocols — the universal language of the internet. TCP guarantees delivery (breaks data into packets, reassembles them). IP handles addressing and routing.
  • OSI Model — 7 layers (Physical, Data Link, Network, Transport, Session, Presentation, Application). Know the order and the mnemonic: All People Seem To Need Data Processing (7→1).
  • Internet = public, anyone can access. Intranet = private, employees only (same technology, different access). Extranet = extends intranet to selected partners. The technology is the same — access control differs.
  • VPN encrypts your data through the public internet — used for remote work and privacy. Firewall filters traffic to block attacks. Bandwidth = network capacity measured in bps.
