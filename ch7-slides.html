Chapter 7 · Definitions
E-Commerce vs. E-Business
These terms are often used interchangeably — but they are not the same thing. Click each to see the distinction.
🛒
E-Commerce
Buying and selling goods or services over the internet. The transaction itself — placing an order, processing payment, receiving a digital product.

Examples: Buying shoes on Nike.com. Streaming a movie on Amazon Prime. Paying for Spotify. — Any commercial transaction online.
Click to reveal
🏢
E-Business
The broader use of internet technology to run ALL business processes. Not just transactions — includes operations, supply chain, HR, customer service, internal communication, everything.

E-commerce is a subset of e-business. All e-commerce is e-business — but not all e-business involves a sale.
Click to reveal
🔑
Key Distinction
E-commerce = transaction. E-business = all digital operations.

FIU's online tuition payment = e-commerce. FIU's entire online portal (registration, grades, email, payroll, HR) = e-business. The payment is part of a much bigger digital operation.
Click to reveal
Exam Trap E-commerce ⊂ E-business. E-commerce is just the buying/selling part. E-business is the whole digital enterprise. This is one of the most common Ch 7 exam mistakes.

Chapter 7 · Business Models
E-Commerce Business Models
Who is selling to whom? Click each model to see how it works and real examples.
🏬→👤
B2C
Business-to-Consumer — companies selling directly to individual customers. The most familiar model.
Examples: Amazon, Netflix, Target.com, Airbnb, Uber. Revenue comes from millions of small individual transactions.
Click to reveal
🏬→🏬
B2B
Business-to-Business — companies selling to other companies. Larger transaction values, fewer customers, long-term relationships.
Examples: Salesforce (CRM software to companies), Intel (chips to Dell), Alibaba (manufacturers to retailers). B2B is 5× larger than B2C.
Click to reveal
👤→👤
C2C
Consumer-to-Consumer — individuals selling to other individuals, facilitated by a platform.
Examples: eBay, Craigslist, Facebook Marketplace, Poshmark, Depop. The platform earns fees/commissions; the users do the transacting.
Click to reveal
🏛️→👤
G2C
Government-to-Consumer — government agencies serving citizens online.
Examples: IRS e-filing taxes, DMV online license renewal, FAFSA, benefits portals, paying parking tickets online. Goal is efficiency and accessibility, not profit.
Click to reveal
Also Know B2G (Business-to-Government) — companies selling to the government. Defense contractors, IT vendors, construction firms bidding on government contracts.

Chapter 7 · Team Competition
Business Model Challenge
2 points per correct answer. Identify the e-commerce model for each scenario.
Scenario 1
You download TurboTax, fill out your 1040, and electronically submit it to the IRS through their official portal. Which two e-commerce models are involved in this one transaction?
B2C (TurboTax, a business, selling the software to you, a consumer) + G2C (the IRS, a government agency, receiving and processing your return through their citizen portal). Both models operate in this single workflow.
Click to reveal
Scenario 2
Walmart buys $2 billion worth of packaged goods from Procter & Gamble using an automated ordering system connected to P&G's website. What model is this, and why is B2B typically larger than B2C?
B2B (Business-to-Business) — Walmart and P&G are both companies. B2B is 5× larger than B2C because businesses buy in bulk, have long-term contracts, and each transaction can be worth millions. Fewer deals, much bigger money per deal.
Click to reveal
Scenario 3
You sell your old iPhone on Facebook Marketplace to a stranger across town. Facebook charges you nothing, but shows the buyer ads. What model is this? Who earns money from this transaction and how?
C2C (Consumer-to-Consumer) — you and the buyer are both consumers; Facebook is the platform. Facebook earns revenue through the advertising model — it shows targeted ads to buyers and sellers browsing the Marketplace. The platform facilitates but doesn't own the product.
Click to reveal
Scenario 4
Netflix charges $15.49/month. Spotify charges $10.99/month. Duolingo is free but has a $6.99 "Super" upgrade. Name the revenue model for each. What distinguishes them?
Netflix = Subscription model (pay monthly, access all content). Spotify = also Subscription (free tier is advertising-supported). Duolingo = Freemium (free basic tier + paid premium tier removes ads and adds features). Key difference: Freemium has a functional free tier; pure subscription requires payment for any access.

Chapter 7 · Revenue Models
How Online Businesses Make Money
Every digital company earns money differently. Click each model to see how it works and who uses it.
📅
Subscription
Recurring fee for continued access. Predictable revenue for the company; unlimited use for the customer.
Netflix, Spotify Premium, Microsoft 365, NYT digital
🆓
Freemium
Free basic version + paid premium upgrade. Acquire users free; convert the most engaged ones to paying.
Duolingo, LinkedIn, Dropbox, Zoom (free 40-min limit)
📢
Advertising
Free service to users; advertisers pay to reach them. Revenue from ad impressions and clicks.
Google Search, YouTube (free), Facebook, Instagram, TikTok
🔢
Pay-Per-Use
Pay only for what you use — no subscription required. Scales with usage; low entry barrier.
AWS (cloud compute), Apple Pay per song, Uber per ride, iCloud storage
🔗
Affiliate
Earn a commission for referring customers to another company's product or service.
Amazon Associates, travel bloggers, credit card comparison sites, Wirecutter
Exam Note Know all five models and be able to identify them from a scenario. Most companies use a mix — Google uses advertising + pay-per-use (Google Cloud) + subscriptions (YouTube Premium).

Chapter 7 · EDI
Electronic Data Interchange (EDI)
The original B2B e-commerce — developed in the 1960s, still processing trillions in transactions today. The backbone of global supply chains.
Feature 📄 Traditional B2B (Paper) ⚡ EDI (Electronic)
Process Print purchase order → mail or fax → manually re-enter at supplier → print invoice → mail back Automated computer-to-computer exchange in standardized format. No human re-entry required.
Speed Days to weeks Seconds to minutes
Error rate High — manual re-entry creates mistakes Near-zero — machines read standardized formats exactly
Cost ~$50–100 per paper transaction (labor, printing, postage) ~$1 per EDI transaction
Example Small retailer faxing an order form to a vendor Walmart's system automatically orders from P&G when shelf inventory drops below threshold
Key Point EDI isn't new — it pre-dates the web by 30 years. But it remains critical because it eliminates human error in high-volume B2B ordering. Walmart, Target, and almost every major retailer still runs on EDI.

Chapter 7 · Payments
Digital Wallets & Online Payments
The payment layer is what makes e-commerce possible. Click each payment technology to see how it works.
📱
Digital Wallet
A software-based system that securely stores payment information. Eliminates re-entering card details for every purchase.
Examples: Apple Pay, Google Pay, Samsung Pay, PayPal, Venmo. Linked to credit cards, debit cards, or bank accounts. Also stores loyalty cards, tickets, boarding passes.
Click to reveal
🔒
SSL/HTTPS
Secure Sockets Layer / HTTPS — encrypts data in transit between your browser and a web server. The padlock in your browser = SSL active.
Never enter a credit card on a site without HTTPS. SSL certificates are required by law for any site handling payment data (PCI-DSS compliance).
Click to reveal
💳
Payment Gateways
Third-party services that process card transactions for merchants.
Examples: Stripe, Square, PayPal, Authorize.net. When you click "Pay Now," a gateway encrypts your card data, contacts the card network, gets approval/decline, and returns the result — all in ~2 seconds.
Click to reveal
₿
Cryptocurrency
Decentralized digital currency using blockchain technology. No bank or government controls it.
Bitcoin, Ethereum are most common. Still limited in e-commerce use due to volatility, but growing. Some retailers (Overstock, Newegg) accept Bitcoin. Primarily used for investment and speculation currently.

Chapter 7 · Strategy
E-Commerce Success Factors
Why do some e-commerce sites thrive while others fail? Click each factor to see why it matters.
🏪
Digital Storefront
Your website IS your store. Load speed, mobile optimization, clear navigation, and search functionality directly impact conversion rates. 53% of mobile users abandon a page that takes more than 3 seconds to load. Amazon A/B tests every button color.
Click to reveal
🛒
Shopping Cart & Checkout
Friction kills conversions. 70% of shopping carts are abandoned — usually because checkout is too long or forces account creation. Best practice: one-page checkout, guest option, auto-fill, multiple payment methods. Amazon's 1-Click patent was worth billions.
Click to reveal
🔗
Supply Chain Integration
E-commerce only works if the back-end works. Real-time inventory, warehouse management, and shipping integration must sync with the front-end store. When you see "In Stock," that's supply chain data flowing in real-time. Amazon built its logistics network to own this layer.
Click to reveal
⭐
Trust & Personalization
Customers buy from sites they trust. Reviews, ratings, secure checkout badges, clear return policies build trust. Personalization — recommendations based on browsing history — increases average order value by 15–25%. "Customers also bought..." = data-driven selling.

Chapter 7 · Challenges
E-Commerce Challenges
Every opportunity creates risks. Click each challenge to understand why e-commerce is harder than it looks.
🔐
Security & Fraud
Credit card fraud, identity theft, data breaches, phishing. $41B in e-commerce fraud losses in 2022. Businesses must invest in encryption, fraud detection algorithms, and PCI-DSS compliance. Every hack erodes customer trust permanently.
Click to reveal
👁️
Privacy Concerns
E-commerce companies collect massive amounts of behavioral data. Customers are increasingly aware — and uncomfortable. GDPR (EU), CCPA (California) require consent and data transparency. "If the product is free, you are the product" — behavioral data has enormous monetary value.
Click to reveal
⚖️
Legal & Tax Issues
Which laws apply when a Florida company sells to a customer in France? Jurisdiction is complex. Sales tax collection varies by state — the Supreme Court's 2018 South Dakota v. Wayfair ruling now requires most online retailers to collect state sales tax. Intellectual property, contracts, and disputes all vary by jurisdiction.
Click to reveal
🌍
Global Competition
E-commerce removes geographic barriers — but so does it for your competitors. A small Miami boutique now competes with Shein (China), ASOS (UK), and Amazon simultaneously. Disintermediation — cutting out middlemen — lets manufacturers sell direct to consumers, disrupting traditional retail entirely.

Chapter 7 · Takeaways
What You Must Know
  • E-Commerce = buying/selling online (the transaction). E-Business = all digital business operations (broader). Every e-commerce transaction is part of an e-business — but not all e-business involves a sale.
  • Business models: B2C (business → consumer), B2B (business → business, 5× larger), C2C (consumer → consumer via platform), G2C (government → citizen). Also B2G (business → government).
  • Revenue models: Subscription (recurring fee), Freemium (free + paid tier), Advertising (ad-supported free service), Pay-Per-Use (metered billing), Affiliate (commission for referrals). Most companies mix models.
  • EDI (Electronic Data Interchange) = computer-to-computer B2B transactions in standardized format. Pre-dates the web. Eliminates human error, cuts transaction costs from ~$75 to ~$1. Still runs most major supply chains.
  • Digital wallets store payment info securely (Apple Pay, PayPal). SSL/HTTPS encrypts transactions. Payment gateways (Stripe, Square) process cards in ~2 seconds. Never transact on a non-HTTPS site.
  • E-commerce challenges: security/fraud, privacy laws (GDPR/CCPA), complex tax jurisdiction, global competition, disintermediation. Future: m-commerce (73% of traffic), social commerce, AR shopping, AI personalization.
