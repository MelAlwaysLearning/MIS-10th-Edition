What Is IT Ethics?

🔒
Privacy
The right to control who sees your personal information. Violated when companies collect data without consent or share it without permission.

Chapter 3 · Team Activity
PII or Not PII?
Team challenge — 2 points per correct answer. Fastest team wins the round.
Scenario 1
Netflix tracks which movies people in Miami watch most. They use this in aggregate reports. Is this PII?
NOT PII — aggregate data with no individual identifiers is not personally identifiable. If Netflix stored YOUR watch history tied to YOUR account, that IS PII.
Click to reveal
Scenario 2
A hospital stores a patient's name, date of birth, diagnosis, and insurance ID in their system. PII?
YES — PII (and protected by HIPAA). All four fields individually or combined can uniquely identify this patient.
Click to reveal
Scenario 3
A grocery store tracks that "customers who buy diapers also buy beer on Fridays." Is this PII?
NOT PII — this is an aggregated behavioral pattern. No individual is identified. But if this data is linked to a specific loyalty card account, THEN it becomes PII.

Scenario 4
An app collects your real-time GPS coordinates every 30 seconds and stores them with your username. PII?
YES — PII. Precise location data tied to a username creates a detailed personal profile and can identify where you live, work, worship, and receive medical care.

Chapter 3 · Privacy Law
GDPR — Europe's Privacy Shield
General Data Protection Regulation — the world's strongest data privacy law (EU, 2018)
✍️
Informed Consent
Companies MUST get clear, specific consent before collecting personal data. Pre-checked boxes and vague terms don't count.
Click to reveal
🗂️
Right of Access
You can demand to see ALL data a company holds about you. They must provide it within 30 days.
Click to reveal
🗑️
Right to be Forgotten
You can request permanent deletion of your personal data. The company must comply unless they have a legal reason to keep it.
Click to reveal
💸
Massive Fines
Up to €20 million OR 4% of global annual revenue — whichever is higher. Google was fined €50M, Amazon €746M.
Click to reveal
Scope GDPR applies to ANY company serving EU citizens — including US companies like Google and Meta.

Chapter 3 · US Law
US Privacy Laws
Each law protects a different sector. Click to learn what each one covers.
HIPAA
Health Insurance Portability & Accountability Act
Protects personal health information in healthcare settings. Hospitals, doctors, and insurers cannot share your medical records without consent.
Click to reveal
FERPA
Family Educational Rights & Privacy Act
Protects student education records. Schools cannot release your grades, transcripts, or disciplinary records without your consent (after age 18).
Click to reveal
COPPA
Children's Online Privacy Protection Act
Restricts websites from collecting data on children under 13. Requires parental consent before any personal data is collected from minors.
Click to reveal
CCPA
California Consumer Privacy Act
US's closest equivalent to GDPR — California residents can opt out of data sales, know what is collected, and request deletion.
Click to reveal
Exam Trap GDPR is NOT a US law. HIPAA ≠ student records (that's FERPA). Each law = one sector.

Chapter 3 · Case Studies
When Ethics Failed
Click each case to see what went wrong and what it cost
📘 Facebook / Cambridge Analytica
A quiz app harvested 87 million users' data without consent and used it to target political ads.
$5B FTC Fine — Largest ever at the time. Violated user privacy by sharing PII with third parties without informed consent.
🏨 Marriott Hotels
A data breach exposed 500 million guests' passport numbers, emails, and credit card data — for 4 years before detection.
£18.4M UK Fine (GDPR) — Violated data integrity and availability. Guests were not notified promptly.
🚗 Uber
Uber suffered a breach of 57 million users' data — then paid hackers $100K to stay silent and hid it from regulators for a year.
$148M Settlement — Violated breach disclosure laws. Cover-up made it dramatically worse than the breach itself.
🧬 23andMe
Genetic data from 6.9 million users was accessed in a breach. The data included ancestry and health predisposition information.
Ongoing Litigation — Biometric/genetic data is the most sensitive PII category. Once leaked, it can NEVER be changed.

Chapter 3 · IP Rights
Intellectual Property in IT
Digital content is property. Copying it without permission is theft — even if it's easy.
💻
Software Piracy
Copying, distributing, or using software without a valid license. Costs the industry $46B+ annually. Companies face audits and lawsuits.
Click to reveal
©️
Copyright Violation
Using someone's code, images, music, or text without permission. "I found it on Google" is not a legal defense — most online content is copyrighted.
Click to reveal
📝
Plagiarism
Using someone else's digital content as your own — code, papers, or reports. Academic and professional consequences can be career-ending.
Click to reveal
🔓
Open Source & Creative Commons
Licenses that explicitly allow reuse — often with conditions (credit the author, share-alike, no commercial use). Always check the license.

Real Talk

In a business context: using unlicensed software costs companies millions in fines and legal exposure. One audit can change everything.

Chapter 3 · Takeaways
What You Must Know
  • IT Ethics = standards of right and wrong for collecting, using, and sharing data — four pillars: Privacy, IP, Accuracy, Accessibility
  • PII = data that uniquely identifies you: SSN, address, biometrics, medical records, email. Aggregate or public data is NOT PII.
  • GDPR (EU): informed consent + right of access + right to be forgotten + fines up to 4% of global revenue
  • US Laws by sector: HIPAA (healthcare) · FERPA (education) · COPPA (children under 13) · CCPA (California consumers)
  • Ethics ≠ Legal. Something can be legal but still unethical — IS professionals must consider both.
  • Intellectual property includes software, code, and digital content. Piracy and plagiarism carry serious legal and career consequences.
