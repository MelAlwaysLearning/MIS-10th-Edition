Chapter 10 · TPS
Transaction Processing System (TPS)
The foundation of every organization's IS stack. Every sale, paycheck, and order flows through a TPS. Click each to understand what makes TPS unique.
⚙️
What TPS Does
Handles routine, day-to-day business transactions — the high-volume repetitive operations that keep the business running. Sales, order processing, payroll, billing, inventory updates, airline reservations, ATM withdrawals. Speed and accuracy are everything. A TPS processes millions of transactions per day — Visa's network processes 65,000 transactions per second globally.
Click to reveal
🔄
Batch vs. Real-Time
Batch processing: transactions are collected and processed in groups at a scheduled time. Example: payroll runs every two weeks — all time records processed together.
Real-time processing: each transaction is processed immediately as it occurs. Example: ATM withdrawal — your balance updates instantly. Most modern TPS uses real-time — customers expect immediate confirmation. Batch is used when real-time isn't critical (payroll, end-of-month reports).
Click to reveal
🔗
TPS Feeds Other IS
TPS is the data source for MIS, DSS, and EIS. Without accurate TPS data, every other IS fails. The chain: individual sales transactions → TPS captures them → MIS aggregates them into weekly sales reports → DSS models them for forecasting → EIS displays the trend on a CEO dashboard. TPS is the foundation of the entire IS pyramid.
Click to reveal
📍
TPS Examples
Point-of-sale systems (cash register → inventory update → sales record). Online order processing (Amazon confirms → payment processes → warehouse picks → shipping updates). Payroll processing. Bank ATM withdrawals. Hotel reservation system. Airline ticketing. FIU's Panthersoft (student registration, tuition payments). Any high-volume, repetitive business operation = TPS.

Chapter 10 · MIS & DSS
MIS & Decision Support Systems
MIS turns raw data into manager-ready reports. DSS goes further — helping tackle complex, unstructured problems. Click to see the distinction.
📊
MIS — Management IS
Provides structured reports and summaries to support managerial decisions. Takes TPS data, aggregates it, and produces scheduled or on-demand reports.

Examples: weekly sales report by region, monthly inventory status, quarterly employee productivity summary, exception reports (flag items below/above threshold).

Users: middle managers. Decisions: structured (clear criteria, repeatable process). Purpose: monitoring and control.
Click to reveal
🧮
DSS — Decision Support
Provides models and data analysis tools to help managers tackle complex, semi-structured decisions. DSS doesn't make the decision — it provides the tools to analyze options.

"What-if" analysis: if we raise prices by 10%, what happens to volume and profit? Scenario modeling: should we acquire Company X? DSS runs the numbers for each scenario.

Examples: financial modeling tools, route optimization, marketing mix analysis, weather forecasting models.
Click to reveal
🔑
MIS vs. DSS
MIS:
→ Structured decisions
→ Known criteria
→ Produces reports
→ Looks backward (what happened)

DSS:
→ Semi/unstructured decisions
→ Uncertain outcomes
→ Supports analysis
→ Looks forward (what if)

Exam trap: both serve managers — DSS handles the harder, more ambiguous decisions.

Chapter 10 · EIS & ERP
EIS & Enterprise Resource Planning
EIS serves the C-suite. ERP connects the entire organization. Click each to understand these two enterprise-scale systems.
🏆
EIS — Executive IS
Designed for senior executives to monitor KPIs and high-level strategic performance. Aggregates data from MIS, DSS, and external sources into executive dashboards. Emphasizes visualization: charts, gauges, trend lines, alerts.

CEO checks: revenue vs. target, market share, customer satisfaction score, employee turnover. Key feature: drill-down capability — click a high-level number to see the underlying detail. No complex queries required; designed for non-technical executives.
Click to reveal
🏭
ERP — Enterprise Resource Planning
Integrates ALL major business functions into one unified system — finance, HR, supply chain, manufacturing, sales, procurement.

Before ERP: every department had its own system, data was siloed, and information didn't flow between departments. After ERP: one shared database, real-time visibility across the entire company.

Key vendors: SAP (60% market share), Oracle, Microsoft Dynamics. ERP is the backbone of most large organizations.
Click to reveal
⚠️
ERP Implementation Risk
ERP projects are notoriously risky:

🚨 Hershey's (1999): $112M SAP implementation went live before it was stable — couldn't process Halloween orders. Lost $150M in sales.

🚨 Nike (2000): $400M SAP rollout caused $100M in excess inventory and lost orders.

Why? ERP requires changing how every department works. The technology is secondary — the change management is the hard part.
Click to reveal
Exam Alert ERP = Enterprise Resource Planning. It integrates ALL business functions into ONE system. EIS = senior executive dashboards and KPIs. These two often appear on the same question.

Scenario 1
A Target cashier scans 20 items for a customer. The system calculates the total, processes the credit card, updates the inventory, and prints a receipt — all in 3 seconds. What IS type handles this?
TPS — Transaction Processing System. This is a routine, high-speed, repetitive business transaction. No decision-making required — the process is fully structured and automated. TPS speed and accuracy are critical; any delay or error damages customer trust. This same transaction feeds into the MIS that produces daily sales reports.
Click to reveal
Scenario 2
A regional sales manager opens a report showing which of her 15 stores are below their weekly targets, sorted by variance. She uses this to prioritize coaching calls. Which IS type produced this report?
MIS — Management Information System. It produces structured, pre-defined reports from TPS data to support managerial decisions. The manager didn't build the model or run complex analysis — the system generated the report automatically. The decision (which stores to call) is structured: she knows the criteria (below target) and has a clear action (coaching call).
Click to reveal
Scenario 3
McDonald's CFO is evaluating whether to open 200 new locations in Southeast Asia. She uses a tool that models projected costs, revenue under 3 demand scenarios, currency risk, and break-even timelines. Which IS type supports this decision?
DSS — Decision Support System. This is a complex, semi-structured decision with significant uncertainty. The CFO doesn't have a standard report — she needs modeling tools to analyze multiple "what-if" scenarios. DSS supports the decision by running the numbers; it doesn't make the decision. No amount of MIS reports can provide the forward-looking scenario analysis this decision requires.
Click to reveal
Scenario 4
Walmart's CEO opens a real-time dashboard showing global revenue, profit margins, inventory turnover, and customer satisfaction scores across 10,000+ stores — all on one screen, updated every 15 minutes. Which IS type is this?
EIS — Executive Information System. Designed specifically for senior executives: visual KPI dashboards, drill-down capability, high-level strategic view. The CEO doesn't process transactions (TPS), write reports (MIS), or build models (DSS) — they monitor the big picture via dashboards. EIS aggregates data from all other IS types into one executive-level view.

Chapter 10 · Functional Systems
Functional Information Systems
Before ERP, every department had its own IS. These functional systems still exist inside ERP platforms — click each to see how they work.
💰
Accounting IS
Tracks financial transactions, manages accounts payable/receivable, produces financial statements (P&L, balance sheet), handles budgeting and tax reporting. QuickBooks (small business), SAP FI module (enterprise). The financial IS is often the most critical — it must be 100% accurate for regulatory and legal compliance. SOX (Sarbanes-Oxley) requires auditable financial IS controls for public companies.
Click to reveal
👥
HRM — Human Resource IS
Manages the employee lifecycle: recruitment (applicant tracking), onboarding, payroll, benefits administration, performance reviews, training, compliance. Workday, ADP, Oracle HCM are leading HRIS platforms. Modern HRIS includes analytics: voluntary turnover prediction, flight risk scoring (which employees are likely to leave), diversity metrics, compensation equity analysis.
Click to reveal
🏭
Manufacturing IS
Manages production planning, scheduling, quality control, and inventory. Material Requirements Planning (MRP) calculates what raw materials are needed and when. Production scheduling IS: coordinates the factory floor — which machines produce what, in what sequence, at what time. Modern manufacturing IS integrates with IoT sensors on equipment for real-time production monitoring and predictive maintenance.
Click to reveal
📣
Marketing IS
Supports market research, campaign management, customer segmentation, and sales force automation. CRM (Customer Relationship Management) is a key marketing IS. Marketing automation tools (HubSpot, Marketo, Salesforce) automate email campaigns, lead scoring, and customer journey tracking. Marketing IS answers: which campaigns work, which segments convert, what's the ROI of each channel?

Chapter 10 · Business Intelligence
KPIs, Dashboards & Business Intelligence
Modern IS goes beyond reporting — it provides real-time business intelligence to guide decisions at every level. Click each to explore.
🎯
KPI
Key Performance Indicator — a measurable value that shows how effectively an organization is achieving its key objectives. Revenue growth, customer satisfaction score (NPS), employee turnover rate, on-time delivery %, inventory turnover. Good KPIs: specific, measurable, actionable, tied to strategy. Every level of the IS pyramid tracks different KPIs — the EIS shows the CEO's KPIs while the MIS shows a manager's operational KPIs.
Click to reveal
📈
Business Intelligence (BI)
The strategies, technologies, and tools for collecting, analyzing, and presenting business data to support decision-making. BI tools: Tableau, Power BI, Looker, Qlik. Key capabilities: interactive dashboards, data visualization, OLAP (drill-down analysis), ad hoc querying. BI is the layer on top of your IS — it turns raw data from TPS into actionable insights for every level of the organization.
Click to reveal
🗄️
Data Warehouse
A central repository of integrated data from all IS sources — TPS, MIS, external data. Optimized for analysis, not transactions. Data warehouse ≠ database. A transaction database handles one record at a time (speed). A data warehouse holds years of historical data for complex analysis (insight). Amazon Redshift, Google BigQuery, Snowflake are modern cloud data warehouses. Every large company has one.
Click to reveal
🔍
OLAP
Online Analytical Processing — technology that enables users to analyze data from multiple perspectives simultaneously. Cube model: you can slice sales data by product, region, time period, and salesperson all at once. Drill down: start at total company revenue → drill to division → drill to region → drill to individual store → drill to individual transaction. OLAP is the engine behind most BI dashboards and EIS tools.

Chapter 10 · Integration
Why Integration Matters: Breaking Silos
Disconnected IS create information silos — data is trapped in departments and can't flow where it's needed. Click to understand why ERP was invented.
🏚️
The Silo Problem
Before ERP, each department ran its own software: Finance used one system, HR another, Sales a third, Manufacturing a fourth. Data was duplicated, inconsistent, and out of sync. Customer placed an order → Sales had it. Warehouse didn't know. Finance wasn't notified. Shipping had no visibility. The "silo" problem: information is stuck in departmental walls instead of flowing across the organization.
Click to reveal
🔗
ERP as Integration Layer
ERP solves silos with a single shared database — all departments read from and write to the same data. Customer places order → automatically triggers inventory check → warehouse pick order → shipping label → invoice in accounts receivable → sales commission calculation in HR. One input, multiple automatic outputs. No re-entry, no lag, no inconsistency. This is why Fortune 500 companies pay $50M+ for SAP implementations.
Click to reveal
📡
APIs & Modern Integration
Not every company can afford a full ERP. Modern alternative: APIs (Application Programming Interfaces) connect separate systems in real time. Shopify API connects to shipping, accounting, and inventory separately. Salesforce CRM integrates with Marketo (marketing), Zendesk (support), and NetSuite (finance) via API. Modern IS architecture is often a "best-of-breed" approach — specialized tools for each function, connected by APIs.
Click to reveal
🔄
Feedback Loops
Well-integrated IS creates organizational feedback loops — output from one system becomes input for another. TPS generates sales data → MIS compares to targets → alerts manager of underperformance → manager adjusts strategy → updated strategy changes targets in MIS → new TPS data measures against new targets. The IS pyramid isn't a hierarchy — it's a loop. Data flows up AND down constantly to keep the organization adapting.

Chapter 10 · Takeaways
What You Must Know
  • TPS (Transaction Processing System) handles routine, day-to-day business transactions — sales, payroll, orders. Used by front-line workers. High volume, structured, must be fast and accurate. Feeds data to all other IS types. Examples: POS systems, ATM networks, online order processing.
  • MIS (Management Information System) provides reports and summaries to support managerial decisions. Takes TPS data and aggregates it into weekly/monthly reports. Used by middle managers for structured decisions. Examples: sales-by-region report, inventory status summary, exception alerts.
  • DSS (Decision Support System) provides models and data analysis tools for complex, semi-structured decisions. Handles "what-if" scenarios and uncertainty. Used by managers and analysts. Examples: financial models, route optimization, market entry analysis.
  • EIS (Executive Information System) is designed for senior executives to monitor KPIs and strategic performance. Provides visual dashboards with drill-down capability. No complex queries — designed for non-technical executives. Examples: CEO revenue dashboard, CFO profit margin tracker.
  • ERP (Enterprise Resource Planning) integrates ALL major business functions into one unified system with a shared database. Eliminates data silos. Key vendors: SAP, Oracle, Microsoft Dynamics. High implementation risk — Hershey's and Nike both lost hundreds of millions in botched ERP rollouts.
  • Matching IS to user: Cashier → TPS. Middle manager → MIS. Complex decision with uncertainty → DSS. CEO monitoring KPIs → EIS. Whole company in one system → ERP. Know this cold — every exam question gives you a user type and asks for the IS.
