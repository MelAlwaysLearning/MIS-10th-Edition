Chapter 4 · Framework
The CIA Triad
Every security decision in every organization maps back to these three pillars
C
Confidentiality
Only authorized people can access the data. Prevent unauthorized viewing, access, or disclosure.
Example: Encryption, access controls, passwords, role-based permissions
I
Integrity
Data is accurate, complete, and hasn't been tampered with. Ensure data is trustworthy.
Example: Checksums, version control, audit logs, digital signatures
A
Availability
Authorized users can access data and systems when needed. Prevent downtime and disruption.
Example: Backups, redundant servers, disaster recovery plans, uptime SLAs
Exam Trap CIA here stands for Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability — NOT the government agency.

Identify the Attack

Attack A
An employee gets an email from "IT Support" saying their VPN password expired. They click a link and re-enter their credentials on what looks like the company portal.
PHISHING — fake email + fake login page to harvest credentials. CIA: Confidentiality (stolen credentials expose access). Always verify through a separate channel before clicking.

Attack B
A hospital system wakes up to find all patient record files replaced with a message: "Your files are encrypted. Send 50 Bitcoin to restore access."
RANSOMWARE — malware encrypts files and demands ransom. CIA: Availability (system is down) + Integrity (files corrupted). Organizations must have offline backups to recover without paying.

Attack C
An attacker downloads a free game from an unofficial site. When installed, the game also installs software that screenshots the user's screen every 30 seconds and emails them to a server.
TROJAN + SPYWARE — the game is the Trojan (disguised malware). The screenshotter is spyware. CIA: Confidentiality (private information captured and exfiltrated).

SOCIAL ENGINEERING / PRETEXTING — creating a fake scenario (pretext) to manipulate someone into disclosing data. No hacking needed. CIA: Confidentiality (PII disclosed). The MGM Resorts breach used this exact technique.

Chapter 4 · Defenses
The Security Toolkit
Click each control to learn what it defends against
🔥
Firewall
Monitors and filters all incoming/outgoing network traffic based on rules. The "gatekeeper" between your network and the internet. Defends: Confidentiality, Availability.
🔐
Encryption
Scrambles data so only authorized parties with the decryption key can read it. At rest (stored files) and in transit (HTTPS). Defends: Confidentiality.
🔑
Two-Factor Auth (2FA)
Requires two verification types: something you KNOW + something you HAVE or ARE. Even if your password is stolen, the attacker still can't log in. Defends: Confidentiality.
🛡️
Antivirus / Antimalware
Detects, quarantines, and removes known malware signatures. Modern tools also use behavioral analysis to catch new threats. Defends: Confidentiality, Integrity.
🌐
VPN
Virtual Private Network creates an encrypted tunnel for internet traffic. Protects data on public Wi-Fi. Masks your IP address from surveillance. Defends: Confidentiality.
💾
Backups
Regular copies of critical data stored separately (including offline/offsite). The only reliable defense against ransomware — you restore from backup instead of paying. Defends: Availability, Integrity.

Chapter 4 · Authentication
The Three Factors of Authentication
2FA = any two of these three. MFA = multiple factors combined.
🧠
Factor 1
Something You KNOW
Password · PIN · Security question answer · Passphrase

Weakness: Can be guessed, phished, or stolen — and you don't know it's gone.
📱
Factor 2
Something You HAVE
Phone (SMS code) · Authenticator app · Security key (YubiKey) · Smart card

Weakness: Can be lost, stolen, or SIM-swapped (phone number hijacked).
👁️
Factor 3
Something You ARE
Fingerprint · Face scan · Iris recognition · Voice pattern

Weakness: Can't be changed if compromised. Biometric databases are high-value targets.
Real Impact

Microsoft reports that MFA blocks 99.9% of automated credential-based attacks. The Colonial Pipeline breach and Change Healthcare breach both lacked MFA on critical systems.

Chapter 4 · Business Continuity
When Security Fails: BCP & Disaster Recovery
Prevention is the goal. But resilience is the backup plan. Click each card.
📋
Business Continuity Plan (BCP)
A documented plan for how an organization will continue operating during and after a disruption. Covers people, processes, and technology. Answers: "What do we do if our systems go down?"
Click to reveal
⚡
Disaster Recovery (DR)
The technical subset of BCP — specifically how to restore IT systems, data, and infrastructure after an incident. DR includes backup systems, failover servers, and recovery time objectives (RTO).
Click to reveal
🎯
RTO vs RPO
RTO (Recovery Time Objective): How long can we be down? "We must be back online within 4 hours."

RPO (Recovery Point Objective): How much data can we lose? "We can't lose more than 1 hour of transactions."
Click to reveal
🏥
Incident Response
The step-by-step process when a breach happens: Identify → Contain → Eradicate → Recover → Review. Every organization above a certain size is legally required to have an IR plan.
Click to reveal
Real World

Change Healthcare had no BCP covering a ransomware attack. Result: 100M+ patient prescriptions halted for weeks. A plan might not prevent the breach, but it prevents the chaos.

Chapter 4 · Takeaways
What You Must Know
  • CIA Triad: Confidentiality (who sees it) · Integrity (is it accurate?) · Availability (can you access it?). Every security decision maps to one or more of these.
  • 82% of breaches involve a human element. Phishing, social engineering, and insider error are the most common attack vectors — not Hollywood hacking.
  • Attack types: Phishing (deception) · Ransomware (encrypt + ransom) · Trojan (disguised malware) · Spyware (silent surveillance) · DDoS (overwhelm = crash).
  • Defense toolkit: Firewall (gatekeeper) · Encryption (scramble data) · 2FA (two verification types) · Antivirus (detect malware) · VPN (encrypted tunnel) · Backups (ransomware antidote).
  • 2FA = something you KNOW + something you HAVE or ARE. MFA blocks 99.9% of automated attacks.
  • BCP = how the business survives a disruption. DR = how IT systems are restored. RTO = how fast. RPO = how much data loss is acceptable.
