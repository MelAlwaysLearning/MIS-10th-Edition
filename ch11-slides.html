Chapter 11 · CRM
Customer Relationship Management
CRM is the strategy AND the technology for managing every interaction with customers across the entire relationship lifecycle. Click each to explore the core concepts.
🤝
What CRM Is
Customer Relationship Management — managing ALL interactions with customers to build loyalty and maximize value. CRM is both a business strategy and a software platform. The strategy: treat every customer as an individual with personalized service. The software: Salesforce, HubSpot, Microsoft Dynamics CRM — systems that track every customer touchpoint (email, call, purchase, complaint, support ticket).
Click to reveal
🎯
CRM in Action
Key example: personalizing email offers based on purchase history. This IS CRM.

A retailer sees you bought running shoes last month → sends you a personalized email with compression socks and fitness tracker recommendations → you buy.

Other CRM examples: loyalty programs (Starbucks Stars), birthday discounts, automated follow-up after a support call, proactive outreach when a subscription is about to expire. Everything that makes customers feel "known."
Click to reveal
📊
CRM Goals
1. Customer retention — keep existing customers (costs 5× less than acquiring new ones)

2. Upselling — sell a higher-tier product (iPhone 15 → iPhone 15 Pro)

3. Cross-selling — sell related products (laptop → laptop bag + mouse)

4. Reduce churn — identify customers likely to leave and intervene

5. Increase CLV — maximize total lifetime revenue per customer
Click to reveal
⚠️
What CRM Is NOT
Common exam traps — these are NOT examples of CRM:

✗ A company orders raw materials from a supplier (that's supply chain)
✗ A manufacturer ships to a distributor (that's logistics/SCM)
✗ An employee submits an expense report (that's accounting/ERP)

CRM = actions directed at customers to build loyalty and generate revenue. If the action isn't customer-facing, it's not CRM.

Chapter 11 · Customer Value
Customer Lifetime Value & Key CRM Metrics
CRM is driven by data. Click each metric to understand how companies measure and maximize customer value — these are the numbers that drive every CRM decision.
💰
CLV — Customer Lifetime Value
Total revenue a customer generates over their entire relationship with a company.

CLV formula (simplified): Average Purchase Value × Purchase Frequency × Customer Lifespan.

Starbucks CLV: average customer visits 2× week, spends $7, for 20 years → CLV ≈ $14,560.

Why it matters: if CLV is $14,560, spending $500 in marketing to acquire that customer is an excellent investment. CLV tells you how much to spend on acquisition and retention.
Click to reveal
📉
Churn Rate
The percentage of customers who stop doing business with a company in a given period.

If you start the year with 1,000 customers and end with 900, your annual churn rate is 10%.

Netflix's churn rate is ~2% monthly — they fight this by releasing new shows constantly. Even a 1% reduction in churn can add millions to revenue for large companies. CRM systems predict which customers are flight risks so companies can intervene before they leave.
Click to reveal
🎯
Customer Segmentation
Dividing customers into groups based on shared characteristics to deliver more relevant, personalized experiences.

Segments: by demographics (age, location), behavior (purchase frequency, categories), value (high-CLV vs. low-CLV), engagement (active vs. at-risk).

A bank sends different emails to new account holders (onboarding tips) vs. 10-year customers (investment product offers). Segmentation is the engine of personalization.
Click to reveal
Exam Alert CLV = total revenue a customer generates over their entire relationship with a company. Not how long they've been alive. Not their average purchase. The TOTAL lifetime revenue figure.
Chapter 11 · SCM
Supply Chain Management
Before a product reaches you, it touched dozens of companies, countries, and systems. SCM coordinates all of it. Click each to understand the supply chain structure and why it matters.
🔗
What is SCM?
Supply Chain Management = coordinating the flow of goods, information, and finances from raw materials to the end customer — efficiently and cost-effectively.

The supply chain for your iPhone: mines in Congo (cobalt) → factories in China (assembly) → ships across the Pacific → US ports → distribution centers → Apple Store → you.

SCM manages all of this — procurement, production, logistics, distribution, returns — as one integrated system.
Click to reveal
🏭
Supply Chain Structure
Upstream: suppliers of raw materials (steel, silicon, fabric)
Tier 1 Suppliers: direct component manufacturers
Manufacturer: assembles the final product
Distribution: warehouses and distribution centers
Retailers: sell to end consumers
Downstream: customers and returns

Each link is connected by a flow of goods (physical), information (orders, forecasts, tracking), and finances (payments, invoices, credit).
Click to reveal
📊
SCM Goals
The three goals of SCM:

1. Cost efficiency — minimize total supply chain costs (inventory, transportation, waste)

2. Responsiveness — deliver products quickly and reliably to customers

3. Resilience — recover quickly from disruptions (COVID-19, Suez Canal blockage, trade wars)

These goals often conflict: efficiency requires lean inventory (JIT) but resilience requires buffer stock. SCM managers balance these tradeoffs constantly.
Click to reveal
📡
Technology in SCM
RFID: track individual items from factory to store shelf automatically.
IoT sensors: monitor shipping container temperature in real time (critical for pharmaceuticals, produce).
AI demand forecasting: predict how much to order before customers need it.
Blockchain: immutable record of every supply chain transaction — enables instant product authentication and recall management.
Walmart's Food Safety initiative: blockchain reduced food tracing time from 7 days to 2.2 seconds.

Chapter 11 · Inventory
Just-in-Time (JIT) Inventory
JIT transformed manufacturing. Instead of warehousing massive inventory, you receive materials exactly when you need them. Click each to see how it works and why it's both powerful and risky.
⏱️
What JIT Is
Just-in-Time (JIT) = receive materials or goods only when they are needed in production — eliminating excess inventory and storage costs.

Developed by Toyota in the 1950s (Toyota Production System). Core idea: holding inventory is waste. Every box sitting in a warehouse is capital tied up, space consumed, and a spoilage/obsolescence risk. JIT eliminates the warehouse by making the supply chain so reliable that inventory arrives the moment it's needed.
Click to reveal
✅
JIT Benefits
Cost savings: no warehouse space needed, less capital tied up in stock

Quality: defects are caught faster — small batches, quicker feedback

Waste reduction: no overproduction, no expired goods

Flexibility: easier to change production when not locked into a large stockpile

Toyota saves $300M+ annually through JIT practices. McDonald's fries are delivered JIT — potatoes arrive at restaurants 2-3 days before they're cooked.
Click to reveal
⚠️
JIT Risks
JIT has zero buffer — any disruption shuts down production.

2021 semiconductor shortage: car manufacturers using JIT had no chip inventory buffer. Factory shutdowns cost General Motors $2B in lost production.

COVID-19 supply chain collapse: PPE manufacturers using JIT had no stockpile when demand surged 10,000%.

Post-pandemic trend: companies are adding strategic inventory buffers (JIT + safety stock) — accepting higher costs for resilience. "Just-in-case" is replacing pure JIT.
Click to reveal
Exam Alert JIT = receive goods only when needed in production. The goal is to reduce inventory costs. Not to speed up delivery. Not to increase suppliers. Reduce inventory holding costs.

Scenario 1
A customer bought running shoes on Nike.com last month. This week, Nike emails her a 20% discount on running socks with a subject line: "Gear up for your next run." Which concept is this? Why does it work?
CRM in action — personalizing email offers based on purchase history. It works because: 1) relevance (socks logically follow shoe purchase), 2) personalization (the email feels directed at her specifically, not a mass blast), 3) timing (still in her "running gear" buying cycle). This is exactly the definition the exam tests: "A retailer personalizes email offers based on purchase history" = CRM. Cross-selling = selling a related product alongside the original purchase.

Scenario 2
A Toyota assembly line factory orders steel components to arrive every 4 hours — just in time for the next production batch. No excess is stored. What is this strategy called? What is its biggest risk?
Just-in-Time (JIT) inventory. Receive goods only when needed in production — eliminating inventory holding costs. Biggest risk: zero buffer means any supply disruption shuts down production. If the steel delivery is delayed by a strike, weather, or supplier failure, the entire assembly line stops immediately. The 2021 semiconductor shortage exposed this: car manufacturers using pure JIT had no buffer stock and lost billions when chip supplies dried up.

Scenario 3
A Starbucks analyst calculates that a customer who visits twice a week, spends $8 per visit, for an average of 15 years is worth how much total? What metric is this? How should Starbucks use this number?
CLV — Customer Lifetime Value. Calculation: 2 visits/week × 52 weeks × $8 × 15 years = $12,480. Starbucks should use CLV to justify marketing spend: spending $500 to acquire a customer worth $12,480 is an excellent ROI. CLV also justifies loyalty program investment — if the rewards program increases visit frequency from 2× to 3× per week, CLV jumps to $18,720. Every loyalty decision should be evaluated against CLV impact.

Scenario 4
Apple designs the iPhone in California → orders processors from TSMC (Taiwan) → displays from Samsung (South Korea) → assembles at Foxconn (China) → ships to US warehouses → delivers to Apple Stores. What system manages this entire process? Name the three flows it coordinates.
Supply Chain Management (SCM). The three flows it coordinates: 1) Goods flow: physical movement of raw materials, components, and finished products from supplier to customer. 2) Information flow: purchase orders, production schedules, shipping tracking, demand forecasts — data that coordinates every link. 3) Financial flow: payments, invoices, letters of credit — money flowing back upstream as goods flow downstream. SCM's goal: coordinate all three flows efficiently and cost-effectively.

Chapter 11 · SCM Problems
The Bullwhip Effect
Small changes in consumer demand cause massive oscillations upstream in the supply chain — like a bullwhip amplifying a small wrist flick into a huge crack. Click to understand this critical SCM concept.
🪃
How It Works
Consumer demand for toilet paper increases 10% →
Retailer orders 20% more (safety buffer) →
Distributor orders 30% more from the manufacturer →
Manufacturer orders 40% more raw materials →
The small consumer shift became a 4× amplified order upstream.

COVID-19 toilet paper shortage: consumers bought 10% more → stores ordered 50% more → factories ordered 200% more → forests were logged faster than ever. The actual demand increase was tiny — the bullwhip effect created the crisis.
Click to reveal
🔍
Root Causes
1. Demand forecast errors — each company in the chain forecasts independently, not from real customer data

2. Batch ordering — companies order in large batches weekly/monthly instead of continuously

3. Price fluctuations — companies overbuy during promotions and underorder at list price

4. Shortage gaming — when supply is tight, everyone over-orders assuming they'll get less than requested

Solution: share real-time point-of-sale data across the entire supply chain
Click to reveal
💡
SCM Solutions
Vendor-Managed Inventory (VMI): suppliers manage their customer's inventory levels, replenishing automatically. P&G monitors Walmart's diapers inventory in real time and ships exactly what's needed — no bullwhip.

Collaborative Planning, Forecasting, and Replenishment (CPFR): supply chain partners share demand forecasts and replenishment plans — everyone sees the same data.

RFID + IoT: real-time visibility of inventory at every node eliminates information gaps that cause bullwhip.

Chapter 11 · Logistics
Logistics & Order Fulfillment
Logistics is the physical execution of SCM — moving products from where they're made to where they're needed. Click to see how the world's most efficient logistics systems work.
📦
Order Fulfillment
The complete process from order placement to customer receipt: order management → inventory pick → pack → ship → track → deliver → returns. Amazon's fulfillment network: 200+ warehouses in the US, robotic picking systems, same-day delivery in major metros. Amazon's fulfillment advantage is its #1 competitive moat — no competitor can replicate 200M square feet of fulfillment infrastructure built over 20 years.

🚢
Global Logistics
Global supply chains span 150+ countries. A single product may cross 10+ national borders before reaching you. Key chokepoints: Suez Canal (12% of global trade), Panama Canal, Strait of Malacca. 2021 Ever Given Suez Canal blockage: one ship stuck for 6 days held $9.6B in trade daily. Supply chain disruptions can cost the global economy $450B+ in a single event. Geographic concentration is a major risk.

🤖
Automation in Logistics
Amazon Robotics (formerly Kiva): 520,000+ robots in Amazon warehouses move shelves to human pickers instead of humans walking to shelves. Reduces walking time per order by 40%. Autonomous trucks: Waymo Via, Aurora testing self-driving long-haul trucks — $700B US trucking industry target. Drone delivery: Amazon Prime Air, Zipline (medical supplies in Rwanda). By 2030, most warehouse picking will be partially automated.

Reverse Logistics
Managing product returns, refurbishment, and disposal — the "reverse" flow from customer back to manufacturer. $890B in US returns annually (2024). Amazon return rate: ~5–20% depending on category. Reverse logistics challenge: each return must be inspected, sorted (resell vs. refurbish vs. recycle vs. dispose), and routed to the right facility. Apple's trade-in program is sophisticated reverse logistics — every returned iPhone is refurbished and resold globally.

Chapter 11 · Takeaways
What You Must Know
  • CRM (Customer Relationship Management) = managing ALL customer interactions to build loyalty and maximize value. Strategy + software (Salesforce, HubSpot). Key actions: personalization, upselling, cross-selling, loyalty programs, churn prevention. CRM is customer-facing — if it's not about the customer, it's not CRM.
  • CLV (Customer Lifetime Value) = total revenue a customer generates over their entire relationship with a company. Not how long they've lived — their total spending across the relationship. CLV justifies acquisition costs and loyalty program investment. High CLV customers deserve more marketing spend and premium service.
  • SCM (Supply Chain Management) = coordinating the flow of goods, information, and finances from raw materials to the end customer. Three flows: physical goods, information (orders/forecasts), and finances (payments). Goal: efficient, cost-effective, resilient coordination of the entire supply chain.
  • JIT (Just-in-Time) = receive goods only when needed in production — eliminating inventory holding costs. Developed by Toyota. Risk: zero buffer means any supply disruption shuts down production. COVID-19 semiconductor shortage exposed JIT's fragility — companies are now adding strategic safety stock.
  • CRM in action (exam answer): A retailer sends personalized email offers based on purchase history. NOT: ordering raw materials (SCM), shipping to a distributor (logistics), or submitting an expense report (ERP/accounting). If it's customer-facing personalization, it's CRM.
  • Bullwhip effect: small consumer demand changes amplify upstream into massive supply chain overreactions. Caused by independent forecasting, batch ordering, and information gaps. Solution: real-time POS data sharing across the supply chain (Walmart-P&G vendor-managed inventory model).
